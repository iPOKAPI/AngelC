Shaping the Teacher Within: A Journey of the Road Less Traveled

A.Lesson Plan Making



Stepping into the role of a student teacher intern in a multigrade classroom was both thrilling and intimidating. Multigrade teaching was a world I had only read about, and now I was living it—an environment where students of different grade levels, abilities, and needs shared one space. On my first day, I was overwhelmed by questions: How do I balance teaching multiple levels? How do I ensure each student is learning? Am I truly ready for this challenge?


The first struggle I had lay in lesson planning. Crafting lessons that addressed multiple grade levels at once felt like solving a complex puzzle. Each lesson needed to be engaging, differentiated, and tailored to meet the needs of every learner. The pressure to think of appropriate activities and strategies was immense. I would sit at my desk for hours, brainstorming ideas, searching for inspiration, and trying to imagine how each lesson would play out. There were nights when I barely slept. The deadlines emerged over me, and the weight of responsibility pressed harder as I struggled to finish lesson plans on time. The process of preparing each lesson from conceptualizing objectives to designing activities and crafting assessments required a lot of effort. I often found myself starting from scratch, pouring all my energy into creating plans that I hoped would resonate with my students.


Even after hours of working with my lesson plans, the struggle didn’t end. After submitting my lesson plans, minor revisions would be suggested by my mentor. Though the feedback was constructive, it stung a little, knowing that what I had worked so hard on wasn’t perfect. But I understood that these revisions weren’t setbacks, they were opportunities to learn and improve. Slowly, I began to accept that perfection wasn’t the goal; growth was. Despite the sleepless nights, the frustrations, and the moments of self-doubt, I managed to meet the challenges head-on. I developed a routine, learned to manage my time better, and began to trust my instincts. As the days went by, what once felt like an impossible task became more manageable. Lesson planning transformed from a source of frustration into a creative process I began to enjoy. I realized that it wasn’t just about writing objectives or designing activities, it was about envisioning how students would experience the lesson, how they would engage, and how they would learn.


Teaching in a multigrade classroom also taught me adaptability. No matter how carefully I planned, lessons didn’t always go as expected. I had to think on my feet, adjust to the needs of my students, and embrace flexibility. These challenges taught me resilience and resourcefulness—qualities that are invaluable to any teacher. The sleepless nights and countless revisions shaped me into a more capable and confident teacher. I learned that struggles are a natural part of growth and that persistence pays off. I discovered the value of collaboration and feedback and how these can refine not just my work but also my perspective.


This experience in a multigrade classroom has taught me that no challenge is impossible with determination, creativity, and a willingness to learn. It reaffirmed my passion for teaching and strengthened my resolve to make a difference in the lives of my students, no matter the obstacles. Through this journey, I learned that lesson planning is not just about meeting deadlines or ticking boxes. It’s about being thoughtful, innovative, and adaptable.

B. Instructional Materials and Powerpoint Presentation



As a student teacher intern, one of the most challenging yet rewarding parts of my journey was preparing instructional materials and PowerPoint presentations for every lesson and teaching demonstration. Each time I faced the task, I felt the pressure of creating something both meaningful and engaging for my students, all while managing the limited time I had to pull everything together. The preparation often felt overwhelming. Time was never on my side, with deadlines looming and a long list of tasks to accomplish. There were countless moments when I doubted whether I could deliver the kind of quality I envisioned. But despite the struggles, I found ways to manage and thrive. To begin with, I prioritized creativity and functionality in my instructional materials. I made it a point to create materials that were vibrant, visually appealing, and eye-catching, as I wanted them to spark my students’ interest and excitement. I often worked with themes whether it was animals, nature, or a concept tied to the lesson to make the materials more relatable and engaging. These themes allowed me to add a personal and imaginative touch to every visual aid or activity sheet.


I also focused on the practicality of my materials. I ensured that my visual aids were big enough for everyone in the classroom to see clearly. I wanted every child, no matter where they were seated, to feel included and engaged. Beyond size, I made my materials interactive whenever possible, incorporating elements like movable parts, flaps to lift, or spaces for students to write and draw. I believed that learning should be hands-on, and I wanted my materials to encourage active participation. PowerPoint presentations were another significant part of my preparation. Given the limited time, I adopted strategies to work efficiently without compromising on quality. I used templates and saved valuable time by organizing my content systematically. Every slide was carefully thought out, with vibrant designs, clear visuals, and concise text that complimented my verbal explanation.


Managing my time was one of the biggest lessons I learned throughout this process. I created a routine to handle the pressure, allocating specific blocks of time to prepare my materials and breaking the tasks into smaller, manageable steps. By staying disciplined and focused, I was able to meet deadlines without sacrificing the quality of my work. There were nights of little sleep and moments of frustration, but I pushed through, driven by my desire to provide the best learning experience for my students. Every time I stood in front of the class and saw my students’ eager faces as they interacted with my materials, I felt an overwhelming sense of fulfillment. What made this journey even more rewarding was knowing that my efforts paid off. My students were engaged, excited, and actively participating in lessons. Their smiles and enthusiasm reminded me why I chose to become a teacher.

This experience taught me invaluable lessons. I learned that preparation is not just about meeting requirements, it's about creating a connection between the teacher and the learners. I discovered that even in the face of limited time and resources, creativity, and resourcefulness can make a significant impact. Most importantly, I realized that teaching is about going the extra mile to make every lesson meaningful and memorable. The process of preparing instructional materials and presentations may have been demanding, but it was also one of the most fulfilling parts of my internship.

C. Teaching Demonstration



Teaching a multigrade classroom is a challenge.I had to ensure that both groups of learners were engaged and learning effectively, even though their levels and needs varied. It wasn’t easy to manage their attention, especially when I had to focus on delivering a lesson to one group while ensuring the other group stayed on task. In addition, time constraints and the lack of practice often left me feeling unprepared and nervous. Managing two grade levels at the same time was far from easy; it required patience, creativity, and a lot of careful planning. Each day brought new struggles, but it also gave me opportunities to grow, adapt, and develop my skills as an aspiring teacher.


To manage these challenges, I adopted several strategies. First, I created routines for the students to follow. Establishing a clear structure helped them know what to expect, which made the classroom environment more organized and predictable. For example, I designed routines for transitions, group work, and individual tasks, and I made sure to practice these routines with the students until they became familiar.


Classroom management was another significant challenge. With two distinct sets of learners, keeping them focused and engaged simultaneously required careful planning and consistent effort. I quickly realized that without a structured system, the classroom could easily become chaotic. To address this, I established routines and rules that the students could follow and practice daily. These routines, such as lining up for materials, transitioning smoothly between tasks, and taking turns during discussions, created a sense of order and predictability, making the classroom environment more manageable. Building rapport with my students was also crucial. I made an effort to connect with them by using their names, showing genuine interest in their ideas, and creating an interactive learning environment. I incorporated activities that encouraged participation, such as games, storytelling, and group discussions. These strategies not only made the lessons more engaging but also helped maintain their attention throughout the session.


One of the most important aspects of my teaching demonstrations was ensuring that I was confident and well-prepared, even with limited practice time. I learned that mastery of the lesson content was key to delivering it effectively. Before stepping into the classroom, I reviewed my lesson plans thoroughly and anticipated possible questions or challenges. While I didn’t always have time to rehearse, I made sure that I understood the lesson deeply so I could teach it naturally and with confidence. Despite the struggles, there were moments of fulfillment that made all the effort worthwhile. Seeing my students engaged with the lessons and genuinely interested in what I was teaching gave me a sense of pride and accomplishment. Their smiles, questions, and enthusiasm reassured me that I was making a positive impact, even in a challenging setting.


Through these experiences, I learned valuable lessons that will stay with me as I continue my journey in education. I learned that preparation is not just about having materials ready, it’s about understanding your lesson and being flexible enough to adapt to the needs of your students. Confidence and a positive attitude play a huge role in how students perceive you as a teacher, and building that confidence starts with believing in yourself. I learned that teaching is not about perfection but about progress. It’s about finding ways to make learning meaningful and ensuring that every student feels valued and heard. Teaching a multigrade classroom taught me resilience, adaptability, and the importance of connection. I am proud of how I managed to pull everything off despite the challenges. Each teaching demonstration was an opportunity to learn, improve, and grow as an educator. These experiences not only strengthened my skills but also deepened my passion for teaching and my commitment to making a difference in the lives of my students.

D. Periodical Making



Creating my first periodical exam as a student teacher intern was one of the most challenging yet rewarding experiences I’ve had. At first, I had no idea how to even start. The thought of designing an exam for a multigrade classroom, with questions tailored to both Grade 2 and Grade 3 learners, was overwhelming. I worried about how to align the questions with their learning competencies and ensure the test was fair and balanced. The process was frustrating at first, especially with the pressure of deadlines and my inexperience. I spent hours drafting questions, only to second-guess myself repeatedly. The process started with confusion and frustration. These questions flooded my mind: What format should I use? How can I make it balanced? How do I ensure that both grades are fairly assessed without overwhelming myself or the students? I realized quickly that I didn’t just need to test their knowledge; I needed to align the questions with the learning competencies for each grade, incorporate a variety of question types, and make sure the exam was challenging yet achievable for my students. Thankfully, my mentor was there to guide me. She taught me how to structure the test properly, and gave me feedback on my drafts. Her advice on creating clear instructions and diverse question types such as multiple-choice, true-or-false, and short answers helped me improve the quality of my work.


The biggest challenge was managing the multigrade aspect. I had to carefully organize the test so that both grades had distinct yet equally meaningful questions. I also had to consider the varying abilities of the learners and make sure the exam was neither too easy nor too difficult. Despite the struggles and a few revisions along the way, I eventually completed the exam. Administering it to my students was a proud moment, as I saw them engage with the questions I had prepared. This experience taught me valuable lessons about preparation, patience, and persistence. I learned that seeking guidance is essential and that every challenge is an opportunity to grow. Crafting that exam not only improved my skills but also boosted my confidence as a future educator.

C. Incident Reports



Dealing with incident reports in the classroom has been a learning experience that tested both my patience and decision-making skills. Each situation required me to stay calm, think clearly, and ensure fairness while resolving the issue. These moments taught me not just how to handle conflicts, but also how to guide students toward better behavior and understanding. When an incident occurs in the classroom, my first step is to address it immediately but calmly to our mentor. I make sure to gather all the necessary details by listening to everyone involved. I let the students explain their side of the story one at a time, without interrupting them. This not only helps me understand the situation better but also shows the students that their voices are valued.


After hearing everyone out, I carefully analyze the problem and identify what caused it. Some incidents stem from misunderstandings, while others may involve rules being broken or emotions running high. Once I know what happened, I explain to the students involved why their actions had consequences and how those actions affected others. I make it a point to remain fair, ensuring that no one feels singled out or judged unfairly. Depending on the nature of the incident, I decide on an appropriate course of action. If it’s a minor issue, I often resolve it by having the students apologize and reflect on how they could have acted differently. For more serious matters, I document the incident and inform the parents, ensuring transparency and collaboration in addressing the behavior. In every case, I aim to turn the incident into a learning opportunity, helping students understand how to handle similar situations in the future.


One key thing I’ve learned is the importance of staying composed and being consistent with rules. Children look to teachers not just for knowledge, but also for guidance in how to handle challenges.These experiences have taught me that managing incidents in the classroom is not just about solving problems but about teaching life skills. It’s about helping students take responsibility for their actions, understand the impact of their choices, and grow into better individuals. I always remind myself of this simple truth: "Every mistake is a chance to learn." Through each incident report I handle, I am reminded of my role as not just a teacher, but also a mentor guiding my students toward personal growth and better decision-making.

God’s guidance became clear in every situation. I realized that my struggles weren’t just obstacles but opportunities for growth. I learned to trust in His plan, knowing that every difficult moment was shaping me into the teacher I am meant to be. There were days when I wanted to quit, but I was reminded that I was called to this path, and God was there to guide me every step of the way. In the end, my internship was not just about learning teaching strategies and classroom management, it was about learning to trust in myself and in God’s plan for my life. I’ve come to understand that teaching is not just a career but a calling, and that with faith, perseverance, and the support of those around me, I can overcome any challenge.


As I look forward to becoming a full-time teacher, I know that this experience has equipped me with the tools, wisdom, and strength I need to succeed. The Bible verses that have been my guiding light and that have carried me through this journey are Jeremiah 29:11 and Proverbs 3:5. "For I know the plans I have for you," declares the Lord, "plans to prosper you and not to harm you, plans to give you a hope and a future." (Jeremiah 29:11) and "Trust in the Lord with all your heart and lean not on your own understanding." (Proverbs 3:5). These verses remind me that God’s plan for me is good, and that no matter how tough things get, I can trust in Him to guide me through.
